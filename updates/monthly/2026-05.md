# May 2026 Research Summary

Enterprise AI adoption just crossed from "cool experiment" to "procurement checkpoint." The inflection point isn't capability — it's governance. Cursor shipped security agents that run on every PR, Microsoft flipped the switch on Agent 365 (a full control plane for managing agents at scale), and Notion wove agents into private Slack channels. None of these are flashy features. All of them answer the question that's been blocking enterprise deployments: "Can I actually control and audit this?"

The secondary signal is equally important: the economics of building AI products are forcing a reckoning. Harrison Chase (LangChain) is explicitly naming 2026 as the year closed model costs become unsustainable for agentic workloads, Lenny published a forensic breakdown of why SaaS freemium playbooks destroy AI product margins, and PMs at serious companies like Stripe are discovering that vibe coding gets you faster outputs, not better judgment. The pattern connects: as AI tooling matures, the PM's job shifts from "make the AI work" to "make the right call about what the AI should do."

## What Matters

- **Governance is becoming the product, not the overhead.** Cursor's Security Review agents run on every PR automatically (no developer action required); Microsoft Agent 365 bundles identity, audit trails, and compliance controls into a single management plane; Notion's Custom Agents now live in private Slack channels where decisions actually happen. Enterprise buyers aren't asking "does this work?" anymore — they're asking "can I see what it did, who authorized it, and what it cost?" The teams winning enterprise deals this month are the ones shipping audit logs, spend caps, and model allowlists as first-class product features, not compliance theater.

- **The cost structure of AI products is fundamentally different — and copy-paste SaaS playbooks will destroy you.** Lenny's breakdown of freemium monetization shows why: SaaS freemium works because marginal cost approaches zero; AI inference costs are real and scale with usage. The working model isn't flat-rate subscriptions or unlimited free tiers — it's capability-limited free tiers (restrict what the model can do, not just how many times you can do it) paired with usage-based pricing that aligns incentive structures. Companies like Cursor are proving this works at scale by gating Team/Enterprise features (security agents, spend management) and watching consolidation from individual Pro plans upward.

- **Agency beats skills in the AI era, and vibe coding is a leverage tool, not a strategy.** Max Schoening (Notion's Head of Product) made the case explicitly: as AI handles more execution, the PM's durable advantage is judgment and ownership, not technical fluency. Owen Williams vibe-coded Protodash at Stripe and transformed design reviews, but the unlock wasn't the code — it was applying AI to Stripe's specific design system substrate. Wispr Flow shipped a floating scratchpad that intercepts your workflow at the moment of thought (Option + S). The pattern: PMs and individual contributors who build their own leverage tools are outpacing teams that wait for roadmap prioritization.
