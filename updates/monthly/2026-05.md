# May 2026 Research Summary

The line between "PM work" and "engineering work" collapsed this month — not as theory, but as a lived problem teams are actively dealing with. Non-PMs are shipping features directly to production using AI coding tools, Teresa Torres is pushing back on the "product builder" identity at exactly the moment when building has become cheap, and the entire industry is racing to figure out what PMs are actually for when anyone can code. This isn't a future scenario. It's happening now, and it's forcing a renegotiation of authority, process, and value.

The second theme running underneath all of this is governance becoming the actual product. Cursor shipped security agents and model controls. Microsoft launched Agent 365 as a control plane for enterprise agents. Notion learned that users don't trust agents they can't audit. The signal is consistent: as AI agents move from demos to production, the PM's job is increasingly about building the infrastructure that lets organizations *trust* what's happening, not just make it happen faster.

## What Matters

- **AI coding tools are redistributing who can ship, and PMs are losing authority they didn't know was conditional.** Lenny's community is actively debating what to do when engineers use Cursor or Claude Code to ship directly to production, bypassing product reviews. Ryan Nystrom's spec-driven development at Notion shows the new workflow: engineers write detailed specs, agents write code, humans review output. Teresa Torres's push back on the "product builder" identity is pointed — if your PM leverage is "I can code faster," you're competing with AI and losing. The PMs holding durable authority are the ones whose value is discovery, judgment, and "we're building the right thing," not "I shipped it faster."

- **Enterprise AI adoption is stalled on governance, not capability.** OpenAI launched DeployCo (a whole consulting company) because enterprises buy AI access but can't deploy it. Cursor's Security Review agents, Microsoft's Agent 365, and Notion's finding that transparency is table stakes all point to the same problem: procurement and security teams will sign off on AI once you show them audit trails, identity management, and spending controls. The companies that win enterprise aren't the ones with the smartest models — they're the ones who can credibly say "here's exactly what happened, who did it, and why."

- **Vibe coding is converging with agentic engineering in ways that are raising real safety questions.** Simon Willison flagged that the boundary between "casual tool-building" and "autonomous agents taking real actions" is collapsing faster than products are handling it. An app that generates code and an app that writes emails or deletes files look the same to users until they don't. The pattern this creates: users treating agentic capabilities casually because the surface feels low-stakes, then being surprised when the agent takes an autonomous action with real consequences. This is a trust and control design problem that products in this space (Cursor, Lovable, Replit) haven't fully solved.
