# May 2026 Research Summary

The conversation about AI shifted this month from "can it do this?" to "can we actually ship this at scale?" Cursor shipped persistent cloud dev environments and always-on security agents. Notion and Microsoft both launched governance layers for agents. OpenAI launched a whole company (DeployCo) just to help enterprises deploy AI they already bought. The pattern is unmistakable: AI infrastructure is maturing from experiment to production, and the limiting factor isn't capability anymore — it's trust, control, and the operational plumbing enterprises need to say yes.

The secondary shift worth noting: PMs are becoming builders. Teresa Torres ran an engineering sprint to ship AI-generated Opportunity Solution Trees. Owen Williams at Stripe vibe-coded Protodash and transformed how design reviews work. These aren't theoretical examples — they're practitioners with real credibility shipping real things. If you're still waiting for engineers to build your AI ideas, you're probably moving slower than you need to.

## What Matters

- **Agents need environment context to do real work, not just generate output.** Cursor's persistent cloud dev environments, AWS's Lambda-based evaluators, and OpenAI's Codex sandbox all ship the same insight: agents that can access repos, credentials, and build systems can actually close tickets, not just draft solutions. Stripe's payment API for agents and Microsoft's Agent 365 governance layer complete the picture — agents need both the technical infrastructure to act and the control infrastructure to be trusted. This is the threshold that separates "agent demos" from "agents in production."

- **Enterprise adoption unlocks when you solve governance, not when you solve capability.** Cursor gated security review to Teams/Enterprise. GitHub published frameworks for validating non-deterministic agent behavior. Notion shipped an agent retrospective emphasizing that transparency-in-action is table stakes. The pattern across all three: capability matters less than auditability. Teams will use agents that take risky actions if they can see what happened and why. Teams won't use agents that work perfectly but invisibly. If you're building agent features, your audit log is a product feature.

- **The "skills vs. agency" trade is reshaping what PM work actually is.** Max Schoening's framing at Notion, Teresa Torres's engineering sprint, Lenny's monetization piece on why SaaS playbooks fail for AI — all three converge on the same point: as AI handles execution, the PM's value shifts from "can you write a clear spec?" to "can you make good calls under uncertainty?" The practical implication: hiring, development, and compensation for PMs need to optimize for judgment and initiative, not technical depth or delivery precision. This is less obvious than it sounds and worth sitting with.
