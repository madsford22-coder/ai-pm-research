# May 2026 Research Summary

Enterprise AI tooling just crossed a threshold this month — we're watching the transition from "AI is a cool capability" to "AI is infrastructure your security team has opinions about." Cursor shipped always-on security agents that automatically check every PR. Microsoft went GA with Agent 365, a full governance and identity layer for managing agents at scale. Notion pushed its agents into private Slack channels. None of these are flashy features. All of them answer the same question enterprise procurement teams ask: "Can we actually deploy this safely?"

There's a secondary pattern worth noting: the economics of production AI are forcing a reckoning. Harrison Chase, LangChain's CEO, was blunt about it — the cost of closed models is unsustainable for agentic workloads, which means open-source models aren't a future option, they're the 2026 reality. That's reshaping what builders are optimizing for.

## What Matters

- **Enterprise packaging is the new moat.** Cursor gated Security Review to Teams and Enterprise plans, which is smart plan architecture but also a signal about how AI tools differentiate now. The individual productivity layer is table stakes. The enterprise wrapper — governance, audit trails, mandatory deployment across orgs — is where the defensibility lives. Microsoft's Agent 365 is the operating system for that wrapper: centralized identity, compliance trails, security policies that apply across every agent your company deploys. If you're evaluating AI tooling for your org, expect procurement conversations to include "does this integrate with Agent 365?" the same way they asked "does this work with Azure AD?" five years ago.

- **Agent economics are forcing OSS-first architectures.** A single agentic workflow that involves research, planning, execution, and verification can cost 50–200x more in tokens than a simple completion. Harrison Chase saying "the cost of closed models is too high" is LangChain betting that open-source models are now good enough for production agent workloads — and that advantage will compound as those models improve. The implication for product builders: your architecture decisions today (closed vs. open, local vs. API) determine your unit economics in six months. If you're shipping anything agentic, you're already paying this price or you're about to.

- **Meeting users in existing workflows beats building new destinations.** Notion's Custom Agents in private Slack channels removes the friction of switching context to query your knowledge base. Wispr Flow's Scratchpad floats on top of whatever app you're in instead of forcing you to open a notes app. Simon Willison raised the larger pattern: we've solved the app-building problem (vibe coding is fast now), but we haven't solved the discoverability and integration problem. The winning move isn't "build a better note app" or "build a better knowledge tool." It's "be the layer that sits where users already work." That's harder to copy once it sticks.

## Essential Resources

1. **Max Schoening (Notion)** — [Why Cultivating Agency Matters More Than Skills in the AI Era](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/why-cultivating-agency-matters-more) — Notion's Head of Product argues the PM's irreplaceable contribution shifts from executing tasks to owning outcomes, because AI handles execution; also directly critiques vibe coding as shipping more software, not better software.

2. **Microsoft** — [Agent 365 Now Generally Available: Governance, Identity, and Compliance for Enterprise AI Agents](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/security/blog/2026/05/01/microsoft-agent-365-now-generally-available-expands-capabilities-and-integrations/) — The control plane for enterprise agent deployment, integrating identity, audit trails, and security policies into a single management surface that works across agents from any vendor.

3. **Simon Willison** — [We Need RSS for Vibe-Coded Apps](https://simonwillison.net/2026/Apr/30/rss-vibe-coded-apps/#atom-everything) — Identifies the concrete infrastructure gap in the AI app ecosystem: no decentralized discovery or federation standard for lightweight tools, creating both a product opportunity and an internal tooling problem for teams building AI apps.
