# May 2026 Research Summary

Enterprise AI tooling just crossed a meaningful threshold: the features that unlock corporate adoption aren't about making individual developers faster anymore—they're about making security teams comfortable. Cursor shipped always-on security agents, Microsoft embedded a legal agent directly into Word, and the pattern is clear: AI tools that want enterprise revenue need to solve for procurement and compliance, not just velocity.

The other signal worth tracking is that the infrastructure around AI-built apps is still incomplete. As tools like v0, Lovable, and Replit make building trivial, discoverability and sharing have become the real bottleneck—and no one's solved it yet. That's either a real product opportunity or a sign that the "vibe-coded app" moment is overstated.

## What Matters

- **Enterprise adoption unlocks through security and compliance, not speed.** Cursor's Security Review (Teams/Enterprise only) isn't a faster coding feature—it's two always-on agents that automatically check every PR for vulnerabilities and scan codebases continuously. By gating it to team plans, Cursor turns security review into an upgrade forcing function. This is the playbook for the next wave of AI tool sales: ship individual stickiness first, then add the enterprise wrapper that procurement teams will actually approve.

- **Ambient AI capture is the next frontier for voice-first tools.** Wispr Flow's Scratchpad floats on top of every app (Option + S on Mac), letting you dictate notes without context-switching. The follow-up Transforms feature rewrites captured text directly without leaving the scratchpad—rough voice note becomes formatted Slack message in one interface. It's the difference between "AI helps when you remember to use it" and "AI is available wherever you are."

- **Vibe-coded apps need an open discovery layer or they stay internal forever.** Simon Willison identified a real infrastructure gap: tools like v0 and Lovable made building AI apps trivial, but there's no decentralized way to share and discover them. RSS solved this for blogs. Apps don't have an equivalent. If your team has built even one useful script or tool in the last three months, you probably have no system to catalog or share it—that's a concrete problem worth solving before it becomes organizational debt.
