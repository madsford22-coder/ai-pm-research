# May 2026 Research Summary

May was the month AI moved from "exciting capability" to "enterprise infrastructure." OpenAI launched personal finance features in ChatGPT, demonstrating systematic context-layer expansion. Notion shipped a Developer Platform and custom agents in Slack. Microsoft shipped Agent 365 as a control plane. But the real story wasn't the features — it was the governance layer getting built around all of them. Every major AI product this month added something an enterprise security team cares about: audit trails, spending controls, permission models, always-on oversight.

The flip side of this infrastructure maturation is a real reckoning about PM value. Teresa Torres ran an engineering sprint to ship AI-generated OSTs. Stripe's Owen Williams vibe-coded Protodash, transforming design reviews. Non-PMs are shipping directly to production. Max Schoening (Notion) and others are arguing that in an AI era, what matters isn't skills — it's agency and judgment about *what* to build, not *how* to build it.

## What Matters

- **The context-layer play is accelerating faster than incumbents can react.** OpenAI added financial accounts to ChatGPT. Notion extended agents into private Slack channels. Cursor shipped cloud development environments for agents. Each expansion makes the general-purpose tool harder to replace with specialized alternatives. The threat to fintech PMs, productivity tool makers, and vertical SaaS isn't better competition — it's "the AI you already use for everything else now knows your domain too." The moat question shifts from "better features" to "does our product require domain depth that a context-aware general assistant can't replicate?"

- **Enterprise AI adoption has a governance-shaped hole, and products that fill it get the sale.** Cursor's security review agents on Teams/Enterprise tiers, Microsoft's Agent 365 control plane, Notion's admin controls for Custom Agents — none of these make individual productivity better. All of them answer the question: "Who is responsible when this AI does something wrong?" Enterprise buyers care about this more than speed. The build order is: nail individual stickiness first, then add the enterprise wrapper (audit trails, spend caps, model allowlists). Teams shipping governance first without user love fail. Teams with user love who *then* add governance get enterprise deals.

- **The PM value proposition is being renegotiated in real time, and the outcome depends on what you think "PM work" actually is.** Teresa Torres, Max Schoening, and the Lenny community all surfaced the same tension this month: if AI can code, design, and ship, what's the PM for? The answer splitting the industry: either PMs own judgment about what to build (harder to automate, but requires real discovery rigor), or PMs own execution velocity (getting easier to automate away). Teams that treat PM work as "write better specs so agents ship faster" are optimizing for the wrong future. Teams that treat it as "run continuous discovery so we build the right thing" are building durable value.
