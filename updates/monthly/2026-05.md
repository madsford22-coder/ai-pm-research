# May 2026 Research Summary

May was the month when AI moved from assistant to executor — and when the people building products realized this wasn't a tools problem, it was an organizational one. Teresa Torres spent a sprint vibe-coding AI-generated Opportunity Solution Trees. Notion's engineers worked with specs instead of writing code. GitHub's agents ran security reviews on every PR. The pattern across all of it: AI wasn't making people faster at existing jobs. It was fundamentally changing what the job actually was.

The second thread running through May is governance. Microsoft shipped Agent 365 as a control plane for enterprise agents. Cursor added always-on security review and then locked it behind a Teams/Enterprise paywall. Notion published what they learned when agents needed to be trustworthy enough for users to let them run unsupervised. The signal is clear: the companies winning enterprise AI adoption aren't the ones with the smartest models. They're the ones who figured out how to let security teams sleep at night.

## What Matters

- **The bottleneck is no longer "can AI do this?" — it's "can humans trust AI to do this without oversight?"** GitHub published a framework for validating agentic behavior when there's no single right answer. Notion's Custom Agents beta revealed that users won't trust agents unless they can see what they actually did. Cursor shifted to selling security review agents to enterprise, not individual developers. The insight is structural: as agents move from toys to infrastructure, the gatekeepers changed from CTOs to security teams.

- **AI tooling is maturing from individual productivity to organizational infrastructure, with very different product requirements.** Cursor removed the repo requirement from Team Marketplace, letting IT admins standardize MCP servers across an org without involving individual engineers. Microsoft Agent 365 went GA with centralized identity, audit trails, and compliance controls. Sendbird's CEO built an internal adoption program that looks like a game (quests, token leaderboards, skills marketplace) to unlock non-technical teams shipping without engineers. The pattern: the hard problem isn't adoption, it's operational standardization at scale.

- **The PM role is being renegotiated in real time by three simultaneous pressures: vibe coding making shipping frictionless, non-PMs shipping directly to production, and agent economics forcing a rethink of what "value" means.** Teresa Torres and Petra Wille pushed back on the "product builder" identity at exactly the moment when builders-with-AI-tools are shipping to production. Harrison Chase named open-source models as the 2026 bet because closed model costs are unsustainable for agentic workloads. Max Schoening argued that agency (the willingness to own outcomes) matters more than skills (which AI can replicate). The meta-point: if your PM identity is "I ship faster," you're competing with agents. If it's "I decide what to build," you're durable.

Start asking "did the agent stay within the right constraints?" A practical framework for testing agents when there are multiple valid paths to the same outcome.

Users need to see what agents actually did. Context matters more than capability. A first-party post-mortem from building agents at scale that directly applies to anyone shipping agentic features.
