# May 2026 Research Summary

The story of May was infrastructure eating the conversation. While foundation models continued iterating, the real momentum shifted to the plumbing layer — the harnesses, governance systems, and orchestration platforms that make agents actually deployable in production. By month's end, you could see a clear split: companies betting on being the platform layer (Anthropic, Vercel, Cursor) are outpacing those betting on being the smartest model (OpenAI). The 80x growth Anthropic reported tells you where enterprise buyers are already looking.

The tension underneath everything this month was control. Every major shipping had an answer to the same question: who decides what the AI actually does, and can humans see what happened? Security agents in Cursor, governance layers in Microsoft Agent 365, audit trails in OpenAI's internal Codex deployment — these aren't nice-to-haves anymore. They're what make enterprise adoption possible.

## What Matters

- **The harness is the moat, not the model.** Ravi Mehta's analysis of why Anthropic is winning despite OpenAI's smarter models crystallized what was latent all month: the platform layer — the orchestration, memory, tool integration, and reliability patterns — is where real switching costs live. Cursor shipping persistent dev environments, LangChain publishing their agent infrastructure specification, and Notion's Custom Agents retrospective all pointed to the same insight. You can swap models. You can't easily swap harnesses. That reframing changes how you evaluate every "should we build or buy this AI feature" conversation.

- **Autonomy is a dial you have to be explicit about turning.** Simon Willison flagged the dangerous convergence of vibe coding tools and agentic systems — the same interface that generates a quick prototype can now send emails or write to databases, and users don't know the difference. Teresa Torres built AI-generated OSTs herself but immediately surfaced the real risk: the judgment about *what* to build doesn't automate. Notion's Custom Agents beta found that transparency was non-negotiable, even when outputs were correct. The product lesson: when you add autonomous capabilities, make the moment users cross from "generates" to "acts" unmistakably clear. An audit trail isn't a compliance feature — it's a trust feature.

- **Enterprise AI adoption isn't blocked by capability anymore — it's blocked by control and visibility.** Cursor shipping Security Review for Teams/Enterprise, Microsoft shipping Agent 365 governance, and Anthropic's India expansion all signaled the same shift. The conversation with enterprise buyers moved from "does this work?" to "can our security team see what it's doing?" DeployCo (OpenAI's new services arm) is literally a company built around the gap between "we bought access" and "we deployed at scale." For PMs: if you're selling into enterprise, the governance layer isn't a Phase 2 feature. It's what unlocks Phase 1.
