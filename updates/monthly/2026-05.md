# May 2026 Research Summary

The agent infrastructure layer is being built in real time — not as research projects or future roadmaps, but as shipped products this month. AWS AgentCore launched payments infrastructure for agents, Mistral shipped orchestration APIs, LangChain published the harness specs teams actually need, and by month's end, Microsoft had a full governance layer (Agent 365) ready for enterprise deployment. This isn't "AI is coming." It's "agents are already here and teams are scrambling to control them."

The secondary tension emerging this month: as agents move from demos to production, the PM job is quietly being redefined. Dan Shipper's data shows teams growing from 4 to 30 people as AI automation increased — not despite it, because it raised demand for judgment. Teresa Torres ran an engineering sprint to build AI-generated discovery tools. Ravi Mehta introduced the "harness PM" as an actual role. What PMs do is shifting from "ship features" to "direct agents" — and that's a different skill entirely.

## What Matters

- **Agents need environments, not just capability.** Cursor's persistent cloud dev environments, Vercel's sandboxed agent runtimes, and OpenAI's published Codex safety model all converge on the same gap: agents fail at scale when they lack the real operating context of actual work. AWS AgentCore payments and Stripe's agent-powered transactions are the earliest signals that "autonomy" means agents need to execute real actions with real consequences — which demands infrastructure that most product teams don't yet have. The companies shipping this infrastructure first (Cursor, Vercel, AWS) are unblocking entire categories of agentic products.

- **The lock-in moved up the stack — and PMs need to know it.** Ravi Mehta's framing and Harrison Chase's warning are the same insight from different angles: switching models is technically easy, but switching the harness (the orchestration layer, the agent frameworks, the tool-calling conventions) is hard. Teams building on Anthropic's native APIs, OpenAI's Assistants, or proprietary harnesses will find themselves locked in not because the models are good, but because the layer above them is. For any PM evaluating whether to build agent infrastructure, this is the question: are we building on a portable foundation or a proprietary one?

- **Enterprise adoption of AI agents now requires governance theater that most tools aren't shipping.** Microsoft Agent 365 launched as GA, Notion published what they learned about agent autonomy controls during their beta, and Cursor is now shipping always-on security agents gated to enterprise tiers. The pattern is clear: procurement teams won't approve agents without identity management, audit trails, spending controls, and approval gates. Notion found that even correct agents generate support tickets if users can't trace why the agent did what it did. The teams shipping governance layers first (Microsoft, Cursor, Notion) are removing the blocker that's been preventing broader agent adoption.
