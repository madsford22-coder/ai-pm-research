# May 2026 Research Summary

May was the month AI stopped being a productivity feature and became infrastructure—the month when "who controls the agent?" replaced "what can the agent do?" as the real product question. Dan Shipper's data from Every showed that AI doesn't reduce headcount, it raises the floor on what expertise means (4 to 30 employees despite aggressive automation). That's the month's through-line: efficiency gains don't shrink orgs, they expand what's possible, which means the constraint shifted from execution speed to judgment quality.

The secondary pattern this month was the enterprise unlock. Cursor shipped security agents. Microsoft GA'd Agent 365. Notion added Custom Agents to Slack. These aren't new capabilities—they're the organizational plumbing that lets companies actually deploy agents at scale. The individual tools were always good enough; what was missing was the governance, auditability, and compliance layer that procurement teams needed to say yes.

## What Matters

- **AI makes competence cheap, which drives demand for expertise, not less work.** Dan Shipper's report from Every is the clearest data on this: the company grew from 4 to 30 people while automating "every single thing we can" with AI agents. The paradox resolved: when AI handles execution, human judgment becomes scarcer and more valuable. The implication for PMs is uncomfortable—if your value proposition is "I write specs faster," that's not durable. If it's "I decide what's worth building," it is.

- **The harness, not the model, is where lock-in lives now.** Ravi Mehta's analysis of why Anthropic is winning enterprise spend despite OpenAI's better models crystallizes this: OpenAI optimizes for model capability, Anthropic optimizes for platform reliability. That distinction predicts enterprise purchasing better than any benchmark score. The practical consequence: if you're building on AI infrastructure, you can switch models. You can't easily switch harnesses. Knowing where your actual lock-in lives is the difference between a portable product and a hostage situation.

- **Governance is becoming the product, not a compliance tax.** From Cursor's security review agents to Notion's transparency requirements to Microsoft's Agent 365, the month's pattern was clear: teams shipping agents to production need audit logs, approval workflows, and access controls—and they need them built into the product, not bolted on after. The teams winning enterprise deals aren't the ones with the smartest agents. They're the ones whose agents can explain what they did and why.
