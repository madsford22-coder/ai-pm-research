# May 2026 Research Summary

May was the month when "AI makes things faster" stopped being the narrative and "who controls what gets built" became the question. Dan Shipper's data showing Every grew from 4 to 30 employees while automating aggressively, Benedict Evans reframing job displacement as a task-level analysis problem, and Teresa Torres shipping AI-generated Opportunity Solution Trees all pointed at the same thing: the constraint has moved from execution to judgment. This is the month the product conversation shifted from capability to control.

The other thread running through May: infrastructure maturity. Agent frameworks, security governance, enterprise deployment layers, and spend management aren't flashy, but they're the difference between "we demoed an agent" and "we shipped an agent to production." Microsoft's Agent 365 going GA, Cursor shipping security review agents, and Notion's Custom Agents retrospective all signal the same shift — agents are moving from experiments to enterprise infrastructure.

## What Matters

- **The automation paradox is real, and it's not about headcount.** Dan Shipper's Every grew from 4 to 30 people as AI agents automated "every single thing we can," not despite it. The pattern: AI makes expert competence cheap, which drives demand for judgment and framing, which creates more work in a different shape. For PMs, this changes the conversation from "will AI reduce my team?" to "what judgment-intensive work are we actually doing, and do we have enough people who excel at it?" Benedict Evans' framework for job exposure — asking whether the automated task is overhead or core value — is the actual tool for this analysis, not generic "is this job safe from AI?" arguments.

- **The agent harness is the new lock-in vector, and most teams don't realize it yet.** Harrison Chase's observation that "switching model providers is easy, switching harnesses is hard" maps directly to where real switching costs live. LangChain's infrastructure posts on sandboxes, streams, and memory made explicit what was previously implicit: production agents require harness decisions (filesystem access, credentials, state management, event types) that become load-bearing over time. For PMs building on foundation models, the build-vs-buy question on orchestration is as important as the model choice — and that decision compounds faster than anyone realizes.

- **Control surfaces for agents are now table stakes for enterprise buyers, not future roadmap items.** Cursor shipping always-on security review agents, Microsoft's Agent 365 governance layer, and Notion's transparency findings all point at the same buyer objection: "I can't run this if I can't audit it." The organizations shipping agents successfully right now (Notion's Custom Agents, Stripe's agent-powered commerce, Sendbird's adoption quests) have explicitly designed control surfaces — audit logs, effort levels, autonomy dials, approval gates. For product teams building agentic features, the governance layer isn't a Q4 addition; it's the product. Users don't trust agents they can't see inside, even when the agent is correct.
