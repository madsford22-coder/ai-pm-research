# May 2026 Research Summary

May showed us something counterintuitive: AI doesn't reduce headcount when it actually works — it expands scope and creates demand for human judgment that scale can't provide. Dan Shipper's detailed breakdown of Every growing from 4 to 30 employees *because* they automated everything is the month's clearest signal about what happens when productivity gains hit a real product constraint. Meanwhile, Anthropic pulled ahead of OpenAI in enterprise spend despite inferior benchmarks, Cursor shipped infrastructure for persistent agent environments, and the architecture question shifted from "what can AI do?" to "how do we actually deploy this safely?"

The throughline this month: the job mix is reshaping faster than headcount. Teams aren't asking "how do we do more with fewer people?" anymore — they're asking "what judgment calls are genuinely irreplaceable?" and "who's going to pay for the infrastructure that makes agents trustworthy enough to bet the business on?"

## What Matters

- **The rebound effect is real, and it changes the PM question entirely.** Every went from 4 to 30 people as AI made execution cheap, which drove up demand for strategy, framing, and editorial judgment — the human-only work that emerges when commodity work vanishes. This isn't "AI reduces headcount." It's "efficiency gains unlock ambition, and ambition requires people." For PMs modeling AI's impact on team composition, the shift isn't about replacement — it's about what expands when your team can execute 10x faster. Teresa Torres is demonstrating the same pattern: a PM shipping her own AI-generated Opportunity Solution Trees isn't eliminating discovery work, she's accelerating it and uncovering new discovery depth. The economic logic is Jevons Paradox applied to product work: you don't save time, you spend it differently.

- **The enterprise unlock is governance, not capability.** Anthropic is outspending OpenAI in enterprise because Ravi Mehta's analysis nailed it — the platform layer (SDKs, trust posture, developer experience) matters more than model benchmarks for enterprise procurement. Cursor's enterprise moves (Security Review agents, Team Marketplace without repo requirements, model allowlists) and Microsoft's Agent 365 shipping with identity and audit trails aren't nice-to-haves — they're the checklist before procurement ever signs off. Notion's Custom Agents beta found that users wouldn't trust agents they couldn't audit, even when the outputs were correct. This is the pattern: capability gets you noticed, governance gets you deployed. The companies winning this moment are building controls and audit trails as core product features, not compliance afterthoughts.

- **The agent infrastructure layer is maturing from "can we?" to "how do we safely?"** Cursor's cloud dev environments mean agents can actually own full engineering context now — repos, dependencies, credentials, build systems — not just isolated tasks. LangChain published what a production agent harness actually requires (filesystem, sandbox, memory, credential isolation). OpenAI published their own Codex safety model with four controls: sandboxing, approval gates, network policies, and agent-native telemetry. GitHub's validation framework for non-deterministic agent behavior answers the impossible question: "how do you test something where multiple right answers exist?" The signal is synchronized: infrastructure for running agents safely at scale is no longer theoretical. The teams that ship this layer first will own the enterprise AI deployment category.
