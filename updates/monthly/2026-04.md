# April 2026 Research Summary

April opened with a quiet but significant reframing: the inflection point for AI in software engineering wasn't a single product launch, but a convergence that happened in November 2025 when capable models, reliable tool-use, and cheap inference finally showed up at the same time. Simon Willison's "lethal trifecta" framing stuck because it matched what we're seeing everywhere else this month — Stripe shipping 1,300 PRs per week with coding agents, GitHub shipping `/fleet` for parallel agent orchestration, and the general shift from "AI helps me work faster" to "AI *teammates* are now part of how software gets built." This isn't hype. It's infrastructure becoming commoditized.

The second signal running through April is less visible but probably more important for product builders: planning discipline is now the actual bottleneck. Teresa Torres spent six months vibe coding and documented the doom loop that hits you every single time — you skip the spec, the AI generates something, you patch it, it breaks worse, and you start over. The fix isn't better prompting. It's writing a one-paragraph spec before you prompt, reviewing what the AI gives you, and keeping sessions short. That's a PM skill, not a technical skill. And it matters because it suggests the teams that extract real value from AI aren't the ones with the best prompting vocabulary — they're the ones with the strongest planning habits.

## What Matters

- **The economics of software production shifted, not just the tools.** Simon Willison's claim that November 2025 was the actual inflection point is worth sitting with because it's not about AI getting marginally better — it's about the *economics* changing structurally. When Stripe's agents ship 1,300 PRs per week, when GitHub's internal teams can orchestrate multiple agents simultaneously with `/fleet`, when a two-person team can now commit to scope that previously required five, that's a threshold you don't un-cross. For PMs, this means your velocity benchmarks from 18 months ago are outdated, your sprint planning assumptions about team capacity need recalibrating, and the minimum viable scope of an MVP has shifted. This isn't about being faster at the same work — it's about the fundamental ratio of effort to output changing.

- **Product-led growth just got a masterclass in friction removal.** Wispr Flow restructured its pricing to eliminate seat minimums, remove domain restrictions, and drop upfront payment requirements — collapsing four tiers into one. The pattern is textbook but ruthlessly executed: identify the specific blocker that stops your most enthusiastic user from pulling teammates in (seat minimums), remove it entirely, and let the trial do the work. This is how you hit bottoms-up adoption at scale. Every product has an equivalent — whether it's credit card requirements, domain restrictions, manual approvals, or minimum commitments — and Wispr Flow just showed what happens when you actually kill it. The question for your product: what constraint are you defending that's actually killing your viral coefficient?

- **The "floor vs. ceiling" framework separates real AI adoption from hype.** Teresa Torres spotlighting Banani (a canvas-first AI design tool) with the explicit frame of "raising the floor, not the ceiling" cuts through a lot of noise. Tools that let non-specialists do work previously gated on expertise (design, coding, copywriting) are getting traction. Tools that make specialists incrementally better at the same work are harder to measure and slower to adopt. This also surfaces a pattern in how non-engineers are using AI: PMs vibe coding prototypes, engineers exploring design with Banani, founders iterating on flows without waiting on designers. The products winning this transition are the ones enabling new populations to do work, not just automating existing workflows.
