# April 2026 Research Summary

April is the month context persistence stopped being theoretical and became a product problem across every layer of the stack. Teresa Torres is writing about it for individual developers, Microsoft is building it into enterprise Word documents, and Character.AI shipped it as a retention mechanism for consumer relationships. The signal repeats itself: AI is only as useful as what it remembers about you, and right now that knowledge is fragile, siloed, and constantly lost at boundaries.

Underneath these surface announcements is a deeper shift: we've moved from "AI helps you work" to "AI works *with* you in ways that compound." That requires infrastructure most teams don't have yet.

## What Matters

- **Context and skills are load-bearing for team productivity, but almost nobody is sharing them.** Teresa Torres ran Claude Code office hours for months and found a consistent pattern: people build AI workflows on one machine and hit a wall when they switch devices or add teammates. The fix isn't complex—shared config files, CLAUDE.md instruction documents, or version-controlled prompts—but almost no teams have standardized it. The friction point that separates individual AI wins from compounding team wins is invisible because people assume it's just "how it works." Meanwhile, Microsoft Copilot in Word grounds edits in Work IQ (organizational context), and Character.AI's new memory system lets users maintain relationship continuity across sessions. Three companies, same problem, different solutions.

- **The multi-agent coordination problem is now the central product design challenge.** Cursor shipped parallel agents in tiled layouts (April 13), Lenny published a framework for categorizing agent risk, and Maggie Appleton named what everyone's quietly experiencing: when developers run multiple agents simultaneously, those agents have zero awareness of each other—they contradict each other, duplicate work, and leave the developer as the manual integration layer. The interfaces now show agents running side-by-side, but the coordination layer doesn't exist. That's not a UX gap, it's an architectural one. Teams shipping the most interesting solutions right now (LangChain's evals-driven agent improvement, Stripe's "minions" shipping 1,300 PRs/week) have solved this internally, but there's no product that makes this coordination legible to teams building agentic systems.

- **Planning and review discipline, not prompting skill, is what separates successful vibe coding from the doom loop.** Teresa Torres spent six months vibe coding—writing more software than her entire prior career—and published a detailed map of what actually works: write a spec first (even a rough one), review code before accepting it, keep sessions short and scoped. The "doom loop" happens when you skip planning, hit a wall, and start prompting increasingly frantic fixes on top of broken code. This reframes the bottleneck: it's not "can I prompt well enough" but "can I plan and review with discipline." That's a skill PMs already have, which means vibe coding is less a technical skill and more a product discipline. Meanwhile, Keith Rabois argues the PM role itself is being compressed by AI (coordination and translation work disappear), which means the PMs who thrive are the ones functioning as "barrels"—owning outcomes end-to-end—not the ones in the handoff layer.
