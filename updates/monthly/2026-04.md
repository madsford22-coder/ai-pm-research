# April 2026 Research Summary

April is the month the interface changed. Not the capabilities — those have been improving steadily — but how teams actually interact with AI agents shifted from "one assistant answering one question" to "orchestrate multiple agents in parallel." Cursor rebuilt its entire editor around parallel agents, OpenAI moved Codex to pay-as-you-go to remove adoption friction, and Anthropic revealed they're already running autonomous agents (CASH) inside their growth operation. The inflection point isn't coming; according to Simon Willison, we crossed it in November 2025. Now we're seeing the product consequences.

The second thread running through April is about the *actual* work of using AI agents — and it's not what the demos make it look like. The cognitive cost of reviewing agent output is being underestimated. Planning discipline is the real bottleneck, not prompting ability. And the teams getting real value from agentic tools are the ones treating them like they'd treat a junior engineer: with specs, code review, and course-correction mid-task. This is less about AI being smarter and more about PMs needing to build differently.

## What Matters

- **Multi-agent orchestration is now the default interface.** Cursor 3.0 redesigned its entire editor around parallel agents running in different environments (local, cloud, SSH). GitHub Copilot CLI shipped `/fleet` for simultaneous agent tasks. Stripe revealed they're shipping 1,300 PRs per week through autonomous agents. The question for teams isn't whether agents will be part of your workflow — it's how you coordinate multiple of them at once. That changes how you spec work, review outputs, and think about what "done" looks like.

- **Intentional friction is now a growth lever.** Anthropic deliberately added onboarding friction to Claude and grew from $1B to $19B ARR in 14 months. Stripe's data shows agents require completely different checkout UX than humans (no hesitation, no abandoned carts, different error handling). The pattern: removing friction everywhere stops working when you're optimizing for the right users and long-term success. For AI products specifically, friction signals "you need to understand what you're buying" — which filters for users who will actually succeed.

- **Planning and review discipline, not prompting skill, is what separates success from the doom loop.** Teresa Torres spent six months vibe coding and documented a specific failure mode: skip the spec, lose context, get stuck patching patches. The fix isn't better prompts — it's writing a brief before you start, reviewing outputs before accepting them, and keeping sessions short and scoped. Karri Saarinen's Linear framework for human-agent design emphasizes "interrupt and correct" and "transparency over magic." The teams getting value aren't the best at prompting; they're the ones who treat AI like a junior engineer that needs oversight.

## Essential Resources (3)

1. **Karri Saarinen (Linear)** — [How to Design for Human-Agent Interaction](https://every.to/thesis/how-to-design-for-human-agent-interaction) — Six concrete principles for designing agent features that actually work (match autonomy to stakes, preserve user agency, allow mid-task correction).

2. **Teresa Torres** — [Vibe Coding Best Practices: Avoiding the Doom Loop](https://www.producttalk.org/vibe-coding-best-practices/) — Practical patterns from six months of sustained vibe coding, with specific fixes for the failure mode where you skip planning and get trapped in compounding errors.

3. **Simon Willison (via Lenny's Podcast)** — [We've Passed the Inflection Point](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/an-ai-state-of-the-union) — First-person case for why November 2025 marked a structural shift in software development economics, with concrete agentic engineering patterns.
