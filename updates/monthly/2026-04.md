# April 2026 Research Summary

April was the month when "agents doing the work" stopped being theoretical and became the primary interface problem. Stripe shared real checkout data showing AI agents are already transacting in production. Cursor rebuilt its entire UI around parallel agents. OpenAI restructured Codex pricing to remove adoption friction. The signal is consistent: the race isn't about having the best agent anymore — it's about orchestrating many of them without losing your mind (or your data).

There's also a quiet discipline emerging underneath all this. Teresa Torres published a framework for avoiding the "doom loop" in vibe coding (skip planning, get stuck, start over). Simon Willison named the cognitive cost of agent-assisted work — the mental load doesn't disappear, it just shifts from writing to reviewing. Karri Saarinen laid out six principles for human-agent interaction that Linear actually shipped. The pattern: speed without structure is just technical debt that's harder to see.

## What Matters

- **Agent checkout is already real, and it breaks your UX assumptions.** Stripe's analysis of real transaction data shows AI agents are completing purchases on behalf of users right now — not someday. This means your checkout flow needs to work when there's no human reading, no visual hesitation, no retry tolerance. Trust signals matter, but context-specifically: a security badge on a $5 transaction does nothing; on a $500 first-time purchase, it moves conversion meaningfully. The takeaway: test your checkout with an agent as the "user," not a human.

- **The review burden is the new product problem.** As agents take on more execution work, humans shift from producers to reviewers — and nobody has designed that experience well yet. Simon Willison named the cognitive cost; Karri Saarinen's framework for Linear includes "interrupt and correct" as a core principle (most agent UIs only handle happy-path completions); LangChain's self-healing deployment pipeline shows what happens when you automate the review layer entirely. The gap: most products are designed for agents to finish tasks, not for humans to efficiently course-correct mid-task when the agent goes sideways.

- **Planning discipline is the actual bottleneck in AI-assisted work.** Teresa Torres has written more software in six months of vibe coding than her entire prior career — and the pattern that separates success from the "doom loop" isn't prompting skill, it's planning discipline. Spec before you prompt. Review as you go. Keep sessions short and scoped. This is a skill PMs already have. For teams shipping AI-assisted features, the leverage isn't hiring better prompters — it's embedding the planning and review practices that make agents trustworthy enough to actually use.
