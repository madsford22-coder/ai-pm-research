# April 2026 Research Summary

The MVP is dead, and the best product teams haven't noticed yet. Ravi Mehta made the case this month that the minimum viable product was always a constraint-driven pattern — ship lean because building costs money and time. But AI prototyping tools have collapsed that cost to near-zero, which means teams still defaulting to stripped-down v1s are optimizing for scarcity that no longer exists. The real shift isn't about moving faster; it's about moving with higher fidelity from day one.

Underneath this is a bigger pattern: almost every PM practice that got codified in the last decade was designed for a different cost structure. Stripe's Veni Singh showed that checkout UX assumes humans make deliberate decisions — agents don't. Marty Cagan reminded us that internal products follow completely different rules than commercial ones, yet teams keep shipping internal tools with external product discipline. Even the way we think about agents improving has to change: LangChain and Cursor both showed this month that the bottleneck isn't model updates anymore, it's capturing what humans actually care about and feeding that back into the system.

## What Matters

- **The checkout layer is being rewritten by agents and wallets.** Stripe's Veni Singh analyzed real transaction data and found three forces reshaping conversion: AI agents now complete purchases autonomously on Stripe's network (not theoretical, happening in production), digital wallet adoption is accelerating and compressing traditional form-fill checkout, and trust signals matter context-dependently — a badge on a $5 transaction does nothing, but on a first-time $500 purchase it moves conversion meaningfully. If your product touches payments, the checkout UX you built for humans will not work for agents. That's not a future problem.

- **The review burden is the new product problem for agentic workflows.** Simon Willison named the cognitive cost nobody wants to admit: delegating to agents doesn't eliminate mental load, it transforms it into sustained review work that's harder than writing in the first place. Karri Saarinen's six-principle framework (developed from Linear shipping real agent features) gives the concrete answer: agents need "interrupt and correct" workflows, not just happy-path execution. Cursor's redesign around parallel agents and LangChain's self-healing pipeline both point at the same gap — the interface and infrastructure for reviewing multiple agents simultaneously doesn't exist yet, and that's what's actually blocking adoption.

- **Planning and captured knowledge matter more than prompting ability.** Teresa Torres built a vibe coding doom loop framework after six months of writing more code than her entire prior career — the pattern isn't "bad at prompting," it's "skipped planning, then tried to patch broken code." Anthropic revealed they use an internal agent (CASH) to run parts of their growth operation autonomously, and they deliberately added onboarding friction to screen for user quality. LangChain's continual learning post distinguished four learning mechanisms (weight updates, instruction updates, memory updates, tool updates) and made clear that the fastest, most durable improvements happen at the instruction and memory layers, not waiting for model updates. The common thread: teams that get leverage from AI aren't the ones with the best prompting skills, they're the ones with the strongest discovery and planning discipline.

2. **Karri Saarinen (Linear)** — [How to Design for Human-Agent Interaction](https://every.to/thesis/how-to-design-for-human-agent-interaction) — Six concrete principles for agent UX from someone who shipped agent features into a real product; "interrupt and correct" is the most underrated principle for any team building agent workflows.
