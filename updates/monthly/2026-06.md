# June 2026 Research Summary

This month was the inflection point where AI infrastructure stopped being experimental and became table stakes for serious product teams. Microsoft shipped Copilot Cowork to general availability worldwide, AWS dropped a wave of AgentCore features with policy-level guardrails, and Vercel, Stripe, and Notion all made the same strategic move: positioning themselves as infrastructure layers for agent workflows, not just AI feature vendors. The shift is subtle but significant—the conversation moved from "what can AI do?" to "how do we control what AI does at scale?"

The real tension this month emerged around cost and governance. Uber capped Claude Code usage to manage token spend. Ravi Mehta published a framework for cutting AI token costs 10x. Figma introduced pay-as-you-go AI credits instead of flat-rate tiers. Simultaneously, Anthropic had to reverse a policy that would have silently blocked researchers from using Claude, and a US government export control directive suspended access to Fable 5 and Mythos 5 within 24 hours of Vercel's compliance. The pattern is clear: AI features that look free locally become expensive at scale, and dependencies on single vendors carry regulatory and political risk that most PMs haven't fully internalized yet.

## What Matters

- **The infrastructure layer is hardening, and control is the new feature.** Stripe added custom developer controls for agent payment workflows. AWS made guardrails enforceable at policy level, not just inference time. Microsoft's Copilot Cowork shipped with multi-model support so enterprises aren't locked into one path. The pattern across all three: the value isn't in what agents can do anymore—it's in the guardrails that let operators define what agents *should* do. This matters for PMs because it changes how you pitch AI features to risk-averse stakeholders. "Agents that can do X autonomously" is scary. "Agents that can do X within your defined bounds, with audit trails, and with rollback controls" is enterprise-saleable.

- **Token economics are becoming a first-class product problem.** Uber capping Claude Code per-employee, Figma launching pay-as-you-go credits, Ravi Mehta's "tokenmaxxing" framework, and Microsoft's "Tokenomics is the new headcount" framing all converge on the same insight: AI adoption creates runaway costs unless you build cost visibility and constraints into your product from day one. The teams that will win aren't the ones that shipped AI fastest—they're the ones that measured token spend, routed to cheaper models for simple tasks, cached aggressively, and gave operators visible dials to control consumption. If you're shipping an AI feature without a cost model tied to actual ROI, you're building on a time bomb.

- **Distribution and trust are the new differentiation.** Lenny Rachitsky's deep dive into eval-as-PRD, Cursor shipping a Context Usage Report, and Musubi training custom AI for trust and safety all point to the same realization: raw model capability is a commodity now. What matters is whether users trust what the AI is doing. Perplexity deprecated its entire Mac app to go all-in on agentic control. Cassie Kozyrkov published data pushing back on the "AI replaces workers" narrative that's driving bad CEO decisions. The products winning this month are the ones building evaluation, observability, and transparency into their core loop—not as afterthoughts, but as the reason the product exists.
