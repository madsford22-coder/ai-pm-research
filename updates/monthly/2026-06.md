# June 2026 Research Summary

The story of June is control. Everywhere you look, the conversation shifted from "what can AI do?" to "how do we make sure it does the right thing reliably?" Uber capped Claude Code because the best sessions are the most expensive. Figma added checkpoints before generation. LangChain shipped self-evaluation loops. The frontier moved from capability to correctness—and that's going to reshape how every PM thinks about shipping AI features.

The practical consequence is that the companies winning right now aren't the ones with the most capable models. They're the ones giving buyers visible dials: budget caps per team, human review steps before execution, rubrics that catch bad outputs before they matter. Enterprise procurement departments have noticed.

## What Matters

- **Memory is becoming a competitive product problem.** OpenAI's "Dreaming" system for ChatGPT consolidates context instead of just accumulating it—forgetting strategically is as important as remembering. For any PM building features that interact with user history or preferences, this raises a concrete design question: what's your forgetting strategy? Most teams don't have one yet. They just let context windows fill up.

- **AI cost control is now table stakes for enterprise adoption.** Uber hit a ceiling on Claude Code usage and capped it. Figma broke AI credits out of flat pricing tiers with pay-as-you-go options for Professional plans. Cursor added per-team budget controls for Enterprise. The tools that feel most powerful (long agentic reasoning sessions, complex design generation) are the most expensive to run at scale. Every company going broad with AI tools will eventually hit this same wall. The ones that survive are the ones that planned for it.

- **The product boundary is dissolving—agents are becoming first-class citizens.** Perplexity deprecated its entire Mac app to go all-in on an OS-level agent that controls local files. Notion explicitly positioned its new Developer Platform APIs for agents, not just developers. Rippling shipped agents across every product in six months using unified data and LangSmith observability. The question stopped being "should we add AI?" and became "what role does our product play when the agent is the user?" Companies that treat agents as afterthoughts will lose to companies that treat them as the primary interaction model.
