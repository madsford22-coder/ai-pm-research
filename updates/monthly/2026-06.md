# June 2026 Research Summary

June is the month enterprise AI tooling stops being a free-for-all. Uber capped Claude Code because the most valuable sessions are also the most expensive. Cursor shipped multi-team budget controls to unlock procurement sign-off. Figma added a planning step before generation so users can steer before the AI commits. The pattern is consistent: companies that survived the first wave of AI adoption are now building the guard rails that make the second wave possible.

The deeper shift is about what "the product" actually means now. Perplexity deprecated its Mac app to go all-in on OS-level control. Notion explicitly positioned agents as first-class users of its new API. Rippling shipped agents across HR, IT, and finance in six months using unified data as the foundation. The product boundary isn't expanding — it's dissolving. The question for any PM right now isn't "should we add AI?" It's "what role does our product play when the agent is the user?"

## What Matters

- **The cost ceiling is real, and it's closer than you think.** Uber capping Claude Code usage isn't about the tool failing — it's about the economics of long, token-heavy agentic sessions hitting a wall at scale. If your team recommended an AI coding tool rollout in the last six months, you probably don't have a monthly cost monitoring plan yet. You should. The tools that feel most powerful (the ones engineers love most) are exactly the ones that drive unexpected bills. This creates a real tension between adoption velocity and budget reality that's going to hit every company trying to go broad with these tools.

- **Governance as a feature is how enterprise AI gets adopted.** Cursor's multi-team Organizations, with per-team budget controls and separate security policies, is the move that turns a developer power tool into a procurement line item. The objection enterprise IT raises isn't "is it good?" — it's "can we control it?" When vendors can answer that question credibly (separate cost caps, visibility per team, policy controls), deals get signed. Figma's Plan mode in Make follows the same pattern: humans want to see what the AI is about to do before it commits. Checkpoint + review + control = trust.

- **Product boundaries are dissolving in favor of agent-native architecture.** Perplexity replaced its Mac app with an OS-level agent that controls files and apps — not just a better search interface. Notion's Developer Platform explicitly positions agents as first-class users, not an afterthought. Rippling shipped agents across six product areas (HR, IT, finance, payroll, operations) in six months because they'd already solved data unification. The companies winning right now aren't the ones with the most capable models. They're the ones asking: "What if the agent is the primary user, and humans are the exceptions?"
