## Items

### Stripe — Testing Adaptive Pricing Across 1.5M Subscription Checkout Sessions
**Source:** https://stripe.com/blog/product
**Credibility:** High (first-party Stripe blog post authored by Sahil Bhagat, Product Manager, Adaptive Pricing, and Danielle Carollo, Data Scientist, Adaptive Pricing — co-authored by the PM and data scientist who ran the test)

**What happened:** Stripe published results from a production A/B test of their Adaptive Pricing feature across 1.5 million subscription checkout sessions. This is unusually rigorous for an AI product claim — most "AI improves conversion" announcements skip the methodology entirely and lean on vibes or cherry-picked screenshots. Adaptive Pricing dynamically adjusts checkout experience variables (likely pricing display, currency, localization) based on customer signals to optimize subscription conversion. The fact that a PM and a data scientist co-wrote the post is itself a signal: this is product-science work, not marketing copy.

**Key details:**
- 1.5 million checkout sessions — large enough to achieve statistical significance across segments and reduce novelty-effect bias
- Published by Sahil Bhagat (PM) and Danielle Carollo (Data Scientist) — dual authorship suggests the methodology was rigorous enough for a data scientist to put their name on it
- A/B test structure means there's a real control group, not a before/after comparison (which is a much weaker evidentiary form)
- Adaptive Pricing targets subscription checkouts specifically — a high-stakes context where conversion rate differences translate directly to revenue
- Full methodology details are at the URL

**Why it matters for PMs:** This is the template. When a skeptical stakeholder asks "does this AI feature actually work?", the answer shouldn't be "our model says so" — it should look like this: test at scale, publish the methodology, explain what you measured and how. The Stripe post sets an evidentiary bar that most AI feature teams aren't meeting. If you're shipping AI features with claimed conversion, retention, or revenue impact, the question to ask yourself is: could we write this post? If not, you might not know whether the feature works. The co-authorship of PM and data scientist is also a pattern worth noting — PM craft is increasingly about knowing how to design experiments, not just ship features.

**Critical questions:**
- What was the actual lift in conversion rate — the summary mentions results were published but the specific percentage matters enormously. A 0.3% lift and a 12% lift are both "statistically significant" but have very different implications.
- How does Adaptive Pricing handle adverse selection — does it consistently serve optimized checkouts to the right segments, or does it overfit to existing high-converters?
- Was the test run globally or in specific markets, and does the pricing optimization work differently across payment cultures?
- What's the pricing for Adaptive Pricing as a Stripe feature — is this included in standard Stripe fees, or a premium add-on?

**Action you could take today:** Find one AI feature in your product that has a claimed conversion, engagement, or retention benefit that you've never properly A/B tested. Write out what the test would look like — sample size, control group definition, primary metric, guardrail metrics. If you can't write that out in 30 minutes, that's the gap between claiming the feature works and knowing it does.

### Reforge — Discovery Agent: An AI That Asks Before It Builds
**Source:** https://www.reforge.com/blog
**Credibility:** Medium-High (first-party Reforge blog, published March 19, 2026, via Brian Balfour; Reforge is a recognized source for PM craft content, though the full article text is not available — working from title and summary)

**What happened:** Reforge launched a Discovery Agent as part of Reforge Build — described as "a thinking partner that asks before it builds." The positioning is a direct challenge to the vibe-coding default: most AI coding tools interpret a prompt and immediately generate output. The Discovery Agent front-loads clarifying questions before touching code, designed to help teams validate they're building the right thing rather than just building faster. This is a PM craft product, not just a developer tool — it surfaces as Reforge's first significant product-as-AI-tool launch.

**Key capabilities:**
- Acts as a "thinking partner" — not a code generator but a discovery facilitator
- Asks clarifying questions before generating any output
- Positioned inside Reforge Build, Reforge's hands-on product learning environment
- Targets the gap that vibe coding tools create: speed without direction

**Why it matters for PMs:** Reforge is making a product bet that most teams don't actually know what they want to build — they just know how to describe it. The Discovery Agent is a response to a real failure mode: AI coding tools make it very easy to build the wrong thing very quickly. For PMs who've watched "vibe coding" sessions produce polished, functional apps that solve the wrong problem, this is the product artifact that gives product thinking a seat at the table before execution begins. It's also a signal about where Reforge sees the PM craft gap right now: not in delivery speed (AI handles that) but in problem definition. The open question is whether "asking before building" translates to better outcomes, or whether it just adds friction that teams route around.

**Critical questions:**
- What does the Discovery Agent actually ask — is it a fixed set of discovery questions (think opportunity mapping, assumption lists) or does it dynamically adapt to the product context?
- How does it integrate with actual development workflows — does it hand off to a code generation tool after discovery, or is it a standalone ideation step?
- Is this grounded in Teresa Torres-style continuous discovery frameworks (Reforge has her in their network) or is it more generic product thinking scaffolding?
- What's the evidence that front-loading questions improves outcomes — does Reforge have data on this, or is it a hypothesis?

**Action you could take today:** Before your next build session using any AI coding tool, spend five minutes writing down three clarifying questions you'd want answered before building. What user behavior change does this enable? What's the smallest version that tests the core assumption? What would failure look like? Use that as a prompt to your existing AI coding tool before writing any code — it's a manual version of what the Discovery Agent automates, and it tells you whether the concept actually changes your output.

### Mistral AI — Leanstral: Open-Source Code Agent for Formal Verification
**Source:** https://mistral.ai/news/leanstral
**Credibility:** High (first-party Mistral announcement, March 16, 2026)

**What happened:** Mistral released Leanstral — described as the "first open-source code agent for Lean 4." Lean 4 is a formal verification language: rather than testing code with unit tests (which prove the tests pass), formal verification mathematically proves code correctness. Leanstral is a code agent that operates in this formal verification environment, meaning it doesn't just generate code — it generates proofs. The "trustworthy vibe-coding" framing in the subtitle is doing real work here: Mistral is directly positioning this as a response to the reliability gap in AI-generated code.

**Key details:**
- Built for Lean 4, the formal verification language used in high-assurance software (aerospace, cryptography, compilers)
- Open-source — accessible for developers and researchers to build on
- "Trustworthy vibe-coding" positioning is a direct product statement: this is for contexts where AI-generated code that merely works isn't good enough
- First open-source agent of its kind in this space — the "first" claim is significant if it holds

**Why it matters for PMs:** Most AI-generated code is tested informally — run it, see if it breaks, ship it. Leanstral is for contexts where that's not acceptable: safety-critical systems, financial logic, cryptographic protocols. For PMs building in fintech, healthcare, infrastructure, or any domain where a code error has regulatory or safety consequences, this is a signal that the "AI code quality" problem is being attacked from the formal methods direction — not just better models or more testing. It's also a category-creation move: Mistral is carving out a "trustworthy AI coding" niche distinct from raw speed or convenience. If this gains traction, it's the first AI coding product with a mathematically verifiable quality bar.

**Critical questions:**
- How much does Lean 4 expertise is required to use Leanstral — is this accessible to engineers without formal methods backgrounds, or is it a specialist tool?
- What's the practical failure rate — formal verification is powerful but computationally expensive. Does Leanstral actually prove code correct reliably, or does it often produce incomplete proofs?
- Which domains are actually adopting Lean 4 in production today — the tool's value depends entirely on Lean 4 adoption, which is currently niche
- How does Leanstral's approach compare to testing-heavy alternatives (property-based testing, fuzzing) for teams not already using formal verification?

**Action you could take today:** If your product handles financial calculations, cryptographic operations, or any logic where correctness is non-negotiable, identify one function or module that a formal verification approach would most benefit. You don't need to adopt Lean 4 today — but mapping "where would a math proof be better than a test suite" tells you whether Leanstral-style tooling is relevant to your domain, or whether it's a research curiosity for now.
