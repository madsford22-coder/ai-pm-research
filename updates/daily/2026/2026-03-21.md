# Daily PM Research Update: 2026-03-21

## One-Line Summary

Google AI Studio launches a full-stack vibe coding platform with a new Antigravity coding agent, while Perplexity's CEO publicly explains why Comet defaults to Google for iOS search—together revealing how AI platforms are competing on end-to-end developer experience and how new AI browser products navigate the paradox of using incumbents' infrastructure to challenge them.

## Items

### Google AI Studio — Full-Stack Vibe Coding Platform with Antigravity Agent
**Source:** https://blog.google/innovation-and-ai/technology/developers-tools/full-stack-vibe-coding-google-ai-studio/
**Credibility:** High (first-party Google Blog post co-authored by Ammaar Reshi, Product and Design Lead at Google AI Studio, published March 18, 2026)

**What happened:** Google upgraded AI Studio from a model playground into a full-stack development platform. The launch introduces the Google Antigravity coding agent, which enables users to build multiplayer experiences, add databases, and connect to real-world services—all from natural language prompts. This repositions AI Studio from "a place to experiment with Gemini" to "a place to ship full-stack apps with AI." The announcement aligns with Gemini 3 Flash (low-latency, low-cost) launching the same week, which Ammaar described as combining "advanced coding skills, low latency performance, all at a fraction of the cost."

**Key capabilities:**
- **Antigravity coding agent**: Builds multiplayer apps, provisions databases, connects to real-world services from a single prompt—not just frontend scaffolding
- **Full-stack scope**: Backend, database, and external integrations now in scope, not just UI generation
- **Gemini 3 Flash as runtime**: The new Flash model provides the speed/cost profile that makes interactive, iterative vibe coding economically feasible
- **Natural language to deployed app**: The framing is "from prompts to products," positioning AI Studio as competing with Lovable, Replit, and Vercel's v0 for the vibe-coding workflow

**Why it matters for PMs:**
Google entering the full-stack vibe coding space with distribution (AI Studio already has millions of developer users) changes the competitive dynamics for Lovable, Replit, and Cursor. Google's moat here is model access at cost—Gemini 3 Flash running inside an owned platform means Google can offer full-stack AI development at economics competitors can't match without their own model. For PMs deciding where to build AI-assisted development tools, this is a signal that the vibe coding category is consolidating around platforms with integrated model access, not standalone tools with third-party model dependencies. The Antigravity name and full-stack positioning also suggest Google is targeting the "non-developer builds a real app" persona that Lovable has pursued—not just developer productivity.

**Critical questions:**
- How does Antigravity handle the debugging and iteration loop when a generated app breaks—can non-developers actually fix what it ships?
- What's the hosting and deployment model for apps built in AI Studio—does Google own the deployment infrastructure, or do you export to Cloud Run?
- How does full-stack scope (databases, real-world services) affect security and data exposure for apps built by non-technical users?
- Does this cannibalize Firebase or complement it—Google now has two "build apps fast" surfaces with different user profiles?
- Dan Shipper's vibe-coded app (Proof) crashed under load on launch day—does Antigravity do anything to address scalability, or does it just generate the code?

**Action you could take today:**
Build a simple full-stack app in Google AI Studio using the Antigravity agent—specifically one that requires a database and an external service call. Document: how many prompts until it works end-to-end, where it breaks, and whether a non-engineer on your team could have completed the same task. This benchmarks Google's actual capability against the vibe coding alternatives your team already uses.

### Dan Shipper — When Your Vibe Coded App Goes Viral and Then Goes Down
**Source:** https://every.to/chain-of-thought/when-your-vibe-coded-app-goes-viral-and-then-goes-down
**Credibility:** High (first-party case study from Dan Shipper, co-founder of Every, published March 20, 2026—direct post-mortem of a real product incident)

**What happened:** Dan Shipper published a post-mortem on launching Proof—Every's agent-native document editor—which was entirely vibe-coded. At 4 a.m. the night after launch, over 4,000 documents had been created, but the app had been crashing intermittently all day. Dan documents what happened when a purely AI-generated codebase encounters production load for the first time and the developer who shipped it has to debug it. The central observation: debugging a vibe-coded app under fire is a different skill than building one. His conclusion: "If you can vibe code it, you can vibe fix it. You just might not be able to fix it quickly."

**Key patterns documented:**
- **The incident**: Proof launched, went viral, and started crashing under load—a scenario that exposes every assumption baked into a vibe-coded codebase
- **The debugging loop**: Dan walked through iterating with AI to diagnose and fix the crashes—the same workflow that built the app, applied to production debugging under time pressure
- **The asymmetry**: Building with vibe coding is fast because you're making forward progress. Debugging under production load is slower because you're navigating code you don't fully understand, under pressure, with users actively affected.
- **The key finding**: Vibe coding's productivity advantage persists in debugging, but with higher variance—some fixes are fast, others require hours because you don't know what you don't know about the generated code
- **The structural risk**: When the AI generates code you don't understand, you can't form a hypothesis about what's wrong—you can only describe symptoms and hope the AI makes the right diagnosis

**Why it matters for PMs:**
This is the most honest first-person account of vibe coding's production failure modes published by a tracked person. For PMs evaluating AI-assisted development for real products (not internal tools or prototypes), Dan's post-mortem surfaces the risk that isn't in the demos: vibe-coded apps work until they encounter unexpected scale or edge cases, and at that point, the same person who built it fast may not be able to fix it fast. The implication for PM decisions: vibe coding is excellent for proof-of-concept velocity but requires an explicit plan for what happens when it breaks—either a developer who can read the generated code, or an acceptance that debugging will be slow. For PMs managing teams using vibe coding for customer-facing features, this is a forcing function to define your incident response model before something breaks at 4 a.m.

**Critical questions:**
- What's the right quality bar for "vibe coded" versus "engineering-reviewed" based on expected scale and failure cost?
- Does the debugging challenge get better as AI coding tools improve, or is there a fundamental gap between "AI that generates code" and "AI that can reason about why generated code fails under load"?
- How should PM teams categorize vibe-coded features in their risk models—are they prototypes, MVPs, or production code with different SLAs?
- What would a vibe-coding "production readiness checklist" look like, and who owns it—PM, engineering, or the developer who vibe-coded it?

**Action you could take today:**
If your team has shipped anything using vibe coding tools (Lovable, Cursor, v0), identify the one that's closest to customer-facing with real load. Define: what happens if it breaks tonight? Who gets paged? Can they read the generated code? If the answer is "we'd figure it out"—Dan's post-mortem is your forcing function to build that plan before you need it.

### Perplexity — Aravind Srinivas Explains Comet's Google Default Search Strategy
**Source:** https://piunikaweb.com/2026/03/19/perplexity-ceo-explains-comet-default-ios/ (X/@AravSrinivas post, reported via third-party)
**Credibility:** Medium (CEO's direct public statement reported by third-party tech site; original X post not directly accessible but corroborated by multiple sources)

**What happened:** Following Comet's iOS launch, users questioned why Perplexity—a company positioning itself as Google's replacement—defaulted to Google as the search engine on its iOS browser. Srinivas addressed this directly on X, explaining that most smartphone searches are navigational (restaurants, local shops, sports scores, shopping) and Google objectively outperforms all alternatives for those tasks. His framing: "We blend the best of both worlds: Google's navigational speed and breadth of verticals, and Perplexity's answer quality and multimodal experience." He also noted Google is not the default on Comet desktop, and described Comet's native UI as "Safari grade." Separately, he responded to users reporting that the "Hey Plex" wake word was missing on the Galaxy S26, confirmed the team would change it to "Hey Perplexity," and then deleted the post.

**Key product strategy signals:**
- **Pragmatic differentiation, not pure disruption**: Perplexity isn't trying to replace Google everywhere—they're segmenting by query type (navigational vs. answer-seeking) and routing accordingly on mobile
- **Desktop vs. mobile defaults differ**: Different default strategies by platform suggests Perplexity is making deliberate tradeoffs about where their product actually wins versus where Google is better
- **The deleted wake-word post**: Srinivas publicly committed to a product change and then retracted it—either a partnership constraint with Samsung or an internal disagreement that surfaced publicly, then got walked back
- **GTC framing (same week)**: Srinivas described Perplexity at NVIDIA GTC as building "the orchestration system of everything AI can do"—where models are instruments and Perplexity conducts. The Comet default-Google decision fits this framing: Perplexity as orchestrator can route to Google when Google is the right instrument.

**Why it matters for PMs:**
Srinivas's explanation of Comet's Google default is a rare public demonstration of pragmatic product strategy over brand purity. Most companies in Perplexity's position would either pretend Google isn't better at navigational search or try to hide the dependency. Instead, Srinivas publicly explained the tradeoff and positioned it as intelligent routing—a reframe of "we use the competitor's infrastructure" into "we use the best tool for each job." For PMs building products that depend on incumbents' infrastructure or APIs, this is a case study in how to explain the dependency as a feature of your product philosophy, not a weakness. The deleted wake-word post is separately instructive: even CEOs make real-time product commitments on social media that turn out to be constrained—and walking them back publicly creates trust debt.

**Critical questions:**
- If Google is objectively better at navigational search on mobile, what's Perplexity's long-term path to replacing that dependency—or do they plan to maintain it permanently?
- The desktop vs. mobile strategy difference is interesting: what behavioral data is Perplexity using to justify different defaults by platform?
- Does routing users to Google for some queries create a retention risk—do users who regularly see Google-powered results start to re-evaluate whether they need Comet at all?
- The deleted wake-word post suggests either a Samsung partnership constraint or an internal escalation—what does that signal about Perplexity's control over the Galaxy S26 integration?

**Action you could take today:**
Review your own product's dependencies on incumbent platforms or competitors' infrastructure. For each, ask: if a user asked "why do you use [competitor X] for this?"—do you have a clear, public-facing answer that frames the dependency as a product decision? If not, draft that explanation before you're asked—Srinivas's response is a template for how to turn an awkward dependency into a principled product stance.

## This Week's Pattern

**The vibe coding category is bifurcating into "build fast" and "build reliably."** Google AI Studio launches a full-stack agent (Antigravity) that competes directly with Lovable and Replit on generation speed. Dan Shipper's Proof post-mortem reveals that vibe coding's productivity advantage disappears under production failure—debugging generated code you don't understand is slow and high-variance. Geoffrey Litt distinguishes between Claude 3.5 for production code and Claude 3.7 for "yolo vibecoding." Perplexity routes to Google for navigational queries on mobile rather than pretending their product is better everywhere. The pattern: the best practitioners are making explicit tradeoffs about where AI-generated code is good enough versus where engineering rigor is still required—and products that don't make this tradeoff explicit are accumulating invisible risk.
